Critical-Minerals Research

Rare earths have no single price. The seventeen lanthanides-plus trade in opaque, desk-quoted oxide markets where a kilogram of lanthanum costs less than a coffee and a kilogram of terbium costs more than an ounce of gold. Hephaestus resolves that heterogeneity into three transparent, fixed-basket indices — each a different lens on the same question: how exposed is the modern economy to this supply chain, and what is that exposure worth?

Every index below is a proper Laspeyres price index built on the identical estimator. They differ only in the basket — the quantity vector that encodes the weighting philosophy. Methodology

One estimator, three baskets

Each Hephaestus index is a fixed-quantity (Laspeyres) price index — structurally identical to the S&P GSCI or a consumer price index. We hold a representative basket of physical quantities constant and track the changing cost of buying it. Because the basket is fixed, movement in the index is pure price, never a rebalancing artefact.

Ib(t) = 100 × Σi qi(b) · pi(t) Σi qi(b) · pi(t0)
pi(t) = oxide price of element i in month t ($/kg REO, FOB China) · qi(b) = fixed basket quantity for index b · t0 = January 2010 (rebased to 100). The three indices share this equation exactly and differ only in the quantity vector q(b) below.

Choosing q is the entire editorial act. Three vectors, three questions:

QG-SCX · Supply-Chain Criticality
qi = Ci × Si × Ei
Global consumption C tilted by supply concentration S (share of refined output from the single dominant jurisdiction, an HHI proxy) and economic criticality E (end-use value, substitutability, defence relevance). Over-weights the fragile, hard-to-replace links. The flagship — it answers the brief directly.
QG-MAG · Permanent-Magnet basket
qi = Mi (Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb only)
Magnet-sector consumption M, non-zero only for the four NdFeB metals that drive EV traction motors, wind turbines and precision-guided munitions. Everything else is zero. The purest read on where the money and the strategic risk actually concentrate.
QG-VAL · Market-Value / demand
qi = Ci (annual consumption)
Raw global annual consumption C in tonnes of oxide — no tilt. The neutral, cap-weight analogue: what the market physically buys. Dominated in value terms by neodymium, with lanthanum/cerium volume as texture.

Why a fixed basket

Value-weighting with live prices would let a spiking element inflate its own weight and double-count the move (a Paasche bias). Fixing quantities at their structural, consumption-based levels means each index is an honest price signal. The trade-off — weights drift from current market value as prices move — is the correct one for a research benchmark, and it is disclosed rather than hidden.

Data & its limits

There is no live tape for rare-earth oxides. Prices are published periodically by specialist desks behind paywalls. Hephaestus is therefore built on dated public anchor prints — the documented turning points of the market — with monthly values filled by log-linear interpolation between anchors. We deliberately do not fabricate intra-anchor volatility: a straight segment on a log axis is an honest "we did not observe this monthly." The chart's texture is real structure — different elements peaking in different years — not invented noise.

  • Reported — element has a liquid, regularly-quoted oxide market; anchors track published prints closely.
  • Thin — genuine market but illiquid and sparsely quoted; anchors are best-available prints, wider uncertainty.
  • Modeled — negligible traded volume (Sc, Tm); series is a documented estimate, flagged in the table.
  • Promethium (Pm) has no commercial market — radioactive, half-life in years — and is excluded from every index.

Provenance

Where the numbers come from

Anchor prices are reconstructed from public, citable references and cross-checked against the well-documented shape of the market — the 2010–11 export-quota spike, the post-2015 europium collapse as LED phosphors displaced fluorescent lamps, the 2021–22 magnet-metal rally, and the 2023–24 oversupply drawdown.
