A set of rare-earth element price indices
Sebastian Fioravante · data as of —

Rare earths don't really have a single price. The seventeen elements trade as separate oxides in fairly thin, desk-quoted markets rather than on an exchange, and the prices span an enormous range: lanthanum oxide sells for under a dollar a kilogram while terbium oxide runs past a thousand. To make sense of the sector as a whole I built three indices over those oxides. Each one weights the elements differently, so together they give three readings of how the sector has moved.

All three are fixed-basket (Laspeyres) price indices computed the same way. The only thing that changes between them is the basket, the quantity vector that sets the weights. Current levels are in the table below; the figure and the method come after it.

1. Method

Each index is a fixed-quantity (Laspeyres) price index, the same idea as the S&P GSCI or the consumer price index. You fix a basket of physical quantities and track how much that basket costs over time. Because the quantities never change, a move in the index comes only from prices and not from re-weighting:

$$ I_b(t) \;=\; 100 \times \frac{\sum_i q_i^{(b)}\, p_i(t)}{\sum_i q_i^{(b)}\, p_i(t_0)} $$

where \(p_i(t)\) is the oxide price of element \(i\) in month \(t\) (\$/kg REO, FOB China), \(q_i^{(b)}\) is the fixed basket quantity for index \(b\), and \(t_0\) is January 2010 (rebased to 100). The three indices share this equation and differ only in the basket \(q^{(b)}\):

  • QG-SCX (Supply-Chain Criticality, the main one): \(q_i = C_i\, S_i\, E_i\), where \(C_i\) is annual world consumption, \(S_i\) is how concentrated supply is (the refined-output share of the largest producing country, used as an HHI proxy), and \(E_i\) is a measure of economic importance (roughly the value of the end uses, how hard the element is to substitute, and how much it matters for defence). This basket leans toward the elements that are both important and supply-constrained.
  • QG-MAG (Permanent-Magnet basket): \(q_i = M_i\) for Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb and \(0\) for everything else, where \(M_i\) is consumption in the magnet sector. These are the four metals in NdFeB magnets, which go into EV motors, wind turbines and guided munitions.
  • QG-VAL (Market value / demand): \(q_i = C_i\), just annual consumption in tonnes. The plain quantity-weighted version, closest to how much of each element the world actually uses.

Why a fixed basket

If you weighted by live market value instead, an element whose price spikes would gain weight and end up counting its own spike twice (a Paasche-type bias). Keeping the quantities fixed at consumption-based levels avoids that, so each index stays a clean price signal. The cost is that the weights slowly drift away from current market value as prices move. For a benchmark I think that is the right trade-off, and it is worth being upfront about.

2. Data

There is no continuous price feed for rare-earth oxides. Prices get published every so often by specialist desks, and mostly behind paywalls. So the series here are built from dated public prints, at whatever frequency each element actually gets quoted: roughly quarterly to monthly for the liquid magnet oxides (Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb) that dominate the indices, with more detail around the 2010–11 spike and the 2021–22 rally, and much sparser for the thin ones. Months in between prints are filled in by log-linear interpolation. I did not add any fake month-to-month noise, so a straight line between two prints just means there was no quote in between. The wiggles that are there come from different elements moving at different times, not from randomness. How fine the data is depends on the element, which the flag in Table 1 records:

  • reported — an actively quoted oxide market; prints are roughly quarterly to monthly and fairly reliable.
  • thin — a real but illiquid market with sparse quotes, so the series is coarser and less certain.
  • modeled — almost no traded volume (Sc, Tm); the series is an estimate rather than observed prints.
  • promethium (Pm) has no real market, since it is radioactive and decays within years, so it is left out of all three indices.

Disclaimer. This is a personal research project, not investment advice or a live data product. The oxide prices are occasional public prints with interpolation in between, not real-time data, so the absolute levels are approximate, and the thin and modeled series more so. What should hold up is the method and the relative movements, both of which can be reproduced from the description above.
